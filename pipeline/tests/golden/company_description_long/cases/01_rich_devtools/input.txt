their builds on GitHub Actions, GitLab CI, or Jenkins. It ingests every

what they cost, which tests flaked, and where the queue backed up.

How Pipewright works

A lightweight collector runs as a step in your existing pipeline
configuration — no agent daemons, no proxy. It streams timing, cache hits,
artifact sizes, and test results to Pipewright's backend, where a columnar
store keeps 13 months of history. Flake detection replays failing tests in
isolation on ephemeral runners and marks the nondeterministic ones
automatically. Dashboards break down duration by repository, branch, and
team, and alert rules fire on regressions against a rolling baseline.

The collector

The collector is a single static binary, distributed for Linux and macOS
runners, x86 and ARM. It reads the metadata your CI system already emits —
job names, step boundaries, exit codes, runner labels — and pairs it with
what the CI system does not tell you: cache hit ratios per restore key,
artifact upload sizes and durations, and per-test timing parsed from JUnit,
Jest, pytest, and Go test output. Everything is batched and compressed
before upload; a typical build adds 2 to 4 seconds of overhead, most of it
in parallel with teardown. If Pipewright's backend is unreachable, the
collector fails open: your build never blocks on us.

The timeline

Every pipeline run becomes a trace. Jobs are spans, steps are child spans,
and tests are events, so the waterfall view you are used to from
application tracing works on your build: see the critical path, spot the
job that gates everything else, and click from a red build straight to the
failing test's history. The timeline is searchable by any tag — repository,
branch, runner label, author, or a tag you attach yourself in one line of
configuration.

Flake detection

A test that fails and then passes on retry is not fixed — it is a coin you
keep flipping. When a test fails, Pipewright replays it in isolation on an
ephemeral runner, up to three times, with the same commit and environment.
Consistent failure means a real break; inconsistent results mean a flake,
and Pipewright marks it, tracks its failure rate over time, and opens a
quarantine ticket in your tracker if you want one. Teams use the flake
leaderboard in their weekly platform review: the ten tests that wasted the
most engineer-hours, ranked, with links to every occurrence.

Cost attribution

CI spend is a line item nobody can explain. Pipewright meters every job
minute against the runner class that executed it and rolls costs up by
repository, team, and pipeline, so the platform team can answer "what did
the monorepo's integration suite cost last quarter" without a spreadsheet
safari. Budgets and alerts catch runaway matrix builds before the invoice
does. Customers routinely find that a handful of pipelines account for the
majority of spend — and that half of that is cache misses.

Merge queue health

For teams running merge queues, Pipewright tracks queue depth, wait time,
and eviction causes. When the queue backs up, the dashboard shows whether
the culprit is a slow required check, a flaky test evicting batches, or
runner starvation — three problems with three different fixes.

Integrations

GitHub Actions, GitLab CI, and Jenkins are first-class: install is a step
in your workflow file, a keyword in .gitlab-ci.yml, or a plugin from the
Jenkins update center. Test output parsers cover JUnit XML, Jest, pytest,
Go test, and RSpec. Alerts route to Slack, PagerDuty, or a webhook.
Quarantine tickets file to Jira or Linear. Everything Pipewright shows in
the UI is available from a GraphQL API, and a Terraform provider manages
alert rules and budgets as code.

Frequently asked

Does Pipewright slow down my builds? The collector adds 2 to 4 seconds to
a typical build, mostly overlapped with teardown, and fails open if our
backend is unreachable.

Where does my data live? Build metadata and test results are stored in
Pipewright's cloud, encrypted at rest, in a US region. Log payloads stay
in your CI system — we store references, not your logs.

Can I export my data? Yes: the GraphQL API and a nightly Parquet export
on Team and Enterprise plans.

Security

Pipewright is SOC 2 Type II certified. The collector is open source and
auditable, sees build metadata but never your source code, and each
workspace's data is isolated with per-tenant encryption keys. Report
security issues to security@pipewright.dev.

Guide: Sharding a monorepo build with critical-path data

Most monorepo builds are sharded by folklore: someone split the test suite
into eight buckets in 2021 and nobody has rebalanced since. Pipewright's
timeline view shows the actual critical path of your pipeline — the chain
of jobs that determines wall-clock time — and per-test timing shows which
buckets finish early and idle. The guide walks through exporting a week of
test timings, resharding buckets to equalize duration, and setting an alert
on critical-path drift so the balance holds. Teams that reshard on real
timing data typically recover 20 to 40 percent of wall-clock build time
without buying a single extra runner; Brightloop's writeup of their 41-to-14
minute merge-to-green improvement is the canonical example.

Guide: Nightly-release verification

For teams that cut a release every night, the question each morning is
"did last night's build actually verify what we ship?" The guide covers
tagging release pipelines separately from PR builds, alerting on skipped
verification steps, and using artifact size tracking to catch the release
that quietly doubled its bundle. Kestrelworks runs this pattern across
400 nightly jobs.
