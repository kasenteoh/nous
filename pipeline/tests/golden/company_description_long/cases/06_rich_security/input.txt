The problem

Machine identities now outnumber human ones by orders of magnitude, and
they leak: keys hard-coded in repositories, tokens shared across services,
credentials that outlive the engineer who created them. Breach reports
keep tracing incidents back to a forgotten credential.

Human identity got its tooling — SSO, MFA, lifecycle management. The
service account that can read your production database got none of it.
Nobody owns it, nothing expires it, and the only inventory is tribal
memory. Security teams know this; what they lack is a way to see the
problem at its real size and fix it without breaking production.

How Vaultra works

Agentless discovery connects to AWS, GCP, Azure, Kubernetes, GitHub, and
your CI system, then builds an inventory of every credential, where it is
used, and what it can access. A risk engine ranks findings: unrotated keys,
over-privileged tokens, secrets in code. One-click rotation swaps a
credential and updates every consumer through native integrations, with a
rollback window. Policies enforce expiry and least privilege continuously,
not as a quarterly audit.

Discovery

Vaultra connects with read-only credentials to your cloud providers,
clusters, code hosts, and CI — no agents on hosts, no sidecar in every
pod. It enumerates IAM users and roles, service accounts, access keys,
Kubernetes secrets and service-account tokens, GitHub deploy keys and
Actions secrets, database users, and TLS and code-signing certificates.
Usage telemetry — CloudTrail and its equivalents — ties each credential
to the workloads that actually use it, so the inventory answers the three
questions that matter: what exists, what uses it, and what can it touch.
First inventory typically completes the same afternoon you connect.

The risk engine

Every finding is ranked by blast radius and exposure: an unrotated key
with admin scope that a public repository once contained is not the same
as a scoped token rotated last week. The engine flags unrotated keys past
your policy age, over-privileged tokens whose granted permissions exceed
their observed usage, secrets committed to code (with history scanning),
orphaned credentials whose creator has left the organization, and
certificates approaching expiry. Findings come with the evidence attached
— the usage trace, the permission diff — so an engineer can act without
reconstructing the investigation.

Rotation without fear

Inventory tools generate reports; rotation retires risk. Vaultra's
one-click rotation creates the replacement credential, updates every
consumer through native integrations — AWS Secrets Manager, GCP Secret
Manager, Azure Key Vault, Kubernetes secrets, GitHub Actions, your CI's
secret store — validates the consumers are healthy, then revokes the old
credential. A rollback window keeps the old credential revocable-but-
restorable until you close it, so the rotation that breaks an overlooked
consumer is an incident of minutes, not an outage. Rotations can run
one-off, on schedule, or automatically when a finding crosses a severity
threshold.

Continuous policy

Policies turn the cleanup into a steady state: maximum credential age,
mandatory expiry on new tokens, least-privilege enforcement that trims
grants to observed usage after a review period, and no-secrets-in-code
checks wired into pull requests. Drift is surfaced the day it happens.
The quarterly audit becomes a report you export, not a project you staff.

Deployment and security posture

Vaultra deploys as SaaS or in your own cloud account for teams with
data-residency requirements. Discovery credentials are read-only;
rotation uses scoped, audited write permissions you grant per
integration. Every action Vaultra takes is logged to an immutable audit
trail and can be streamed to your SIEM. SSO and role-based access come
standard; secrets material is encrypted with per-tenant keys and never
leaves your chosen region.

Frequently asked

Will rotation break production? The consumer-update-then-revoke sequence
plus the rollback window exists precisely for this. Most teams start with
scheduled rotation on low-risk credentials and graduate to automatic
rotation as confidence builds.

Do you replace our secrets manager? No — Vaultra orchestrates the
managers you already run: Secrets Manager, Key Vault, Kubernetes secrets.
It is the inventory and control plane above them.

What about credentials Vaultra cannot rotate automatically? They stay in
the inventory with owners and expiry tracked, and rotation runbooks are
generated for manual execution.

The first 30 days

Week one is discovery and shock: connect the read-only integrations,
watch the inventory build, and learn the real number — teams that
guessed two thousand credentials routinely find five times that. Week
two is triage from the risk engine's ranking: revoke the orphans nobody
claims, rotate the handful of ancient admin-scoped keys that keep the
CISO up at night, each with the rollback window as the safety net. Weeks
three and four are policy: set maximum ages, turn on expiry for new
tokens, wire the no-secrets-in-code check into pull requests. By day 30
the dashboard trend line — total credentials, median age, findings open
— is the artifact the security lead takes to the risk committee.

For engineers, from the docs

Rotation runs as a plan you can read before it executes: the replacement
credential to be created, the consumers to be updated in order, the
health checks between steps, the revocation at the end, the rollback
path at every stage. Plans are dry-runnable in staging, exportable as
JSON, and every execution is diffable against its plan. The API and
Terraform provider expose the same operations as the console — inventory
queries, policy definitions, rotation triggers — so credential hygiene
can live in the same pipelines as the infrastructure it protects.

More from the FAQ

How long does discovery take to run? The first full inventory
typically completes the same afternoon you connect; incremental
discovery runs continuously after that.

Does Vaultra store our secrets? No. Vaultra inventories metadata and
usage; secret material stays in your secret stores, and rotation
happens through their APIs.

Can developers see their own findings? Yes — findings route to owning
teams with scoped views, so remediation is distributed instead of
bottlenecked on the security team.
