The problem

In-house teams are buried in routine paper. Sales waits days for an NDA;
legal burns evenings on low-risk redlines or ships them to outside counsel
at hundreds of dollars an hour.

The queue is the quiet villain. Every NDA that sits for three days is a
deal that cools, a sales rep who escalates, a GC who gets asked "why is
legal the bottleneck?" — and the paper itself is 80 percent the same
negotiation the team ran last week. Routine review is a volume problem
wearing a judgment costume.

How it works

Your playbook — preferred positions, fallback language, walk-away terms —
is encoded once, with a library of standard positions to start from.
Briefhound's language models classify each clause, compare it to the
playbook, and propose redlines with an explanation attached to every edit.
Lawyers review in Microsoft Word with tracked changes, accept or adjust,
and the playbook learns from what they change. An approvals dashboard shows
cycle times and where negotiations stall.

The playbook

A Briefhound playbook is your team's negotiation posture made executable:
for each clause type — limitation of liability, indemnification, data
protection, payment terms, termination — the preferred position, the
fallbacks you will accept, and the walk-away terms that trigger
escalation. Teams start from Briefhound's library of standard positions
for common paper and edit toward their own posture; most encode their
first playbook in a working session of an afternoon. Playbooks are
versioned, so the position that changed last quarter is documented, not
folklore.

Review in Word, not instead of it

Briefhound works where the redline actually happens. The Word add-in
shows each proposed edit as a tracked change with its explanation in the
margin: which playbook position applies, why this language, what the
fallback would be. The lawyer accepts, adjusts, or rejects — normal Word
review, at speed. Counterparty paper in any format goes in; a clean
tracked-changes document comes out. There is no proprietary editor to
learn and no copy-paste across systems.

The learning loop

When a lawyer edits a proposed redline, Briefhound notices. Consistent
adjustments surface as suggested playbook updates — "you have accepted
this carve-out nine times; make it a fallback?" — so the playbook
converges on the team's real positions rather than its stated ones. The
suggestions are reviewed, never silent: the playbook changes when a
lawyer approves the change.

Intake and the dashboard

Contracts arrive by upload, by email-in, or from the intake form your
sales team submits with the deal context attached. The approvals
dashboard shows every contract in flight: where it is, how long each
stage has taken, which clauses are contested. Cycle-time analytics show
where negotiations stall — the clause that eats a week, the counterparty
type that always escalates — the metric a general counsel actually
reports upward.

Frequently asked

Which documents does it handle? NDAs, MSAs, DPAs, order forms, and vendor
paper — the routine commercial stack. Briefhound is not built for M&A,
employment, or litigation documents.

Does it work on our templates or counterparty paper? Both. Your paper
reviews against your playbook; third-party paper gets classified clause
by clause and redlined toward your positions.

Can it negotiate without a lawyer? It drafts; your team decides. Some
customers auto-send playbook-clean NDAs; most keep a lawyer's scan in
the loop. The escalation rules are yours.

What languages? English-language contracts today, governed by US, UK,
and Canadian law templates.

What the first month looks like

Week one: the playbook working session — your team, our legal
knowledge engineers, an afternoon, and your last fifty negotiated NDAs
as the evidence base. Week two: shadow mode, where Briefhound reviews
incoming paper alongside your normal process and the team compares
output without relying on it. Weeks three and four: live on NDAs, with
MSAs and DPAs following as playbook coverage is approved clause family
by clause family. The dashboard baseline starts on day one, so the
before-and-after cycle times are your own numbers, not our marketing.

The clause library

The library covers the commercial stack's recurring battlegrounds:
limitation of liability with cap structures and carve-outs,
indemnification triggers and procedures, data protection terms mapped
to GDPR and state privacy laws, IP ownership and license grants,
payment terms and late-fee positions, termination rights, assignment,
governing law, and the boilerplate that occasionally bites. Each entry
ships with market-standard positions annotated by deal side — what a
buyer accepts versus what a vendor asks — drawn from the negotiation
patterns of thousands of contracts.

More from the FAQ

How accurate is clause classification? Measured continuously against
lawyer decisions in production; misclassified clauses route to human
review by design, and every explanation cites the playbook position
it applied so errors are visible, not silent.

Do we need to change our templates? No — Briefhound reviews the paper
you have, on your positions. Teams often do tighten templates after
seeing the dashboard's stall-point data, but that is a choice the
data provokes, not a migration.

What does rollout ask of the lawyers? The working session, then
reviewing tracked changes as they already do. There is no new system
to live in — Word is the interface, which is most of why adoption
sticks.
