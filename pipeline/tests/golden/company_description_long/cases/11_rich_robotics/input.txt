Palletmind — One brain for every robot on your floor

It assigns work across AMRs, conveyor systems, and pick stations from

The problem

Warehouses rarely buy all their automation from one vendor. Each robot
brand arrives with its own fleet manager, its own map, its own queue —
and they don't talk. Orders stall while one fleet idles and another
backs up.

Automation was bought in waves: goods-to-person AMRs in 2019, a
conveyor retrofit in 2021, pick-assist robots last year — each rational
alone, each with its own control tower. The result is a floor that is
locally automated and globally uncoordinated, where the bottleneck moves
hourly and no dashboard shows the whole picture.

How Palletmind works

Palletmind connects upward to your WMS — integrations include Manhattan
and Blue Yonder — and downward to each vendor's fleet manager through
its API. A task-allocation solver decides, every few seconds, which robot
should do which job, balancing travel time, battery state, congestion, and
order priority across the whole floor. A shared traffic map prevents
deadlocks at intersections and dock doors. Before go-live, a digital twin
simulates the floor: operators replay their busiest day against a proposed
layout and see throughput before moving a single rack.

The task-allocation solver

Every few seconds, the solver re-plans the whole floor: which AMR takes
which pick, which orders route through the conveyor spur, which pick
station gets the next wave. The optimization balances travel time,
battery state, congestion, and order priority simultaneously — the
nearest robot is not always the right robot if it is at 12 percent
battery or would join a queue at a busy intersection. Wave releases from
the WMS become a continuous flow of tasks, allocated against the floor
as it actually is, not as the morning plan assumed.

The shared traffic map

Each vendor's robots navigate from their own map and cannot see the
others. Palletmind maintains the shared traffic map above them:
intersections, aisle segments, dock doors, and charging areas become
managed resources, with reservations and priorities that prevent the
deadlocks mixed fleets are famous for. When two brands of AMR converge on
the same dock door, one of them yields — deliberately, by policy, not by
bumper.

The digital twin

Go-live fear is rational: handing floor control to new software is how
outages happen. Palletmind's answer is the twin. Before anything goes
live, operators replay their own busiest day — real orders, real
timings — against the proposed configuration and see throughput, robot
utilization, and congestion heat maps before moving a single rack. The
same twin runs what-if questions afterward: another ten AMRs or a second
pick station? A layout change before peak season? The answer comes from
simulation, not from an argument.

The operations console

One screen for the whole floor: live task flow, per-fleet utilization,
congestion, and order-aging alerts. Shift leads see which orders are at
risk and why; engineers see solver decisions with their reasoning
attached. Every allocation is logged and explainable — when a customer
asks why a wave ran late, the answer is a query, not a shrug.

Frequently asked

Does Palletmind replace our fleet managers? No. Each vendor's fleet
manager keeps doing what it does well — navigation, charging, firmware.
Palletmind decides what work each fleet gets and referees the shared
spaces between them.

What if a vendor's API is limited? The integration degrades gracefully:
fleets Palletmind cannot task directly are modeled in the traffic map
and planned around. In practice, every major AMR vendor exposes tasking
APIs — their customers demand it.

Do you support AGVs and ASRS? Conveyor systems and pick stations are
supported today alongside AMRs; ASRS integration is in deployment with
two design partners.

What does the WMS see? Palletmind is invisible upstream: the WMS
releases work as it always has and receives confirmations as if from a
single system.

How the twin is built

The twin is not a generic warehouse simulator with your floor plan
pasted in. It is calibrated from your own telemetry: robot speeds and
turn times observed from the fleet managers, conveyor rates from the
PLC data, pick-station cycle times from the WMS confirmations. Order
replay uses your actual order files — the real mix of singles,
multis, and oversize — so the simulated peak day is your peak day.
Calibration is validated by hindcasting: the twin re-runs last week
and its predicted throughput is compared against what the floor
actually did, with the error published in the go-live report.

Explainability

Solver decisions are inspectable by design. Every task assignment
records the alternatives considered and the costs that decided it —
travel estimate, battery margin, congestion penalty, priority weight.
When an operations manager asks why the far AMR took the near pick,
the console shows the answer: the near robot was two tasks from a
charge threshold and the intersection ahead of it was reserved. Trust
in orchestration is built one explained decision at a time, and the
log is the training material for new shift leads.

Working with robot vendors

Palletmind maintains the vendor integrations as products: when a fleet
manager's API versions, the integration updates centrally, not as a
change order. Vendors cooperate because their robots perform better
inside a coordinated floor — idle fleets embarrass everyone at
renewal time. Integration coverage grows steadily; the current matrix
of supported fleet-manager versions is published in the docs, and new
vendor integrations are scheduled with the customer's deployment, not
after it.

Peak season

The twin earns its keep in September: replay last year's peak against
this year's fleet and layout, test the what-ifs — extra AMRs, a
second shift at the pick stations, a temporary staging zone — and go
into November with a plan that was simulated rather than argued. Sites
report their first orchestrated peak as the moment the platform stops
being an experiment.

More from the FAQ

What happens if Palletmind goes down? Fleets fall back to their own
fleet managers and keep working from their last assignments — the
floor degrades to its pre-Palletmind state, not to a standstill, and
recovery resynchronizes tasking automatically.

How big does a site need to be? The economics start making sense
around two automation vendors or one vendor plus a conveyor system —
coordination is the product, and a single-brand floor already has a
coordinator.

Is the twin available standalone? Yes — some customers run
twin-first engagements to settle a layout or fleet-sizing decision
before committing to orchestration.
