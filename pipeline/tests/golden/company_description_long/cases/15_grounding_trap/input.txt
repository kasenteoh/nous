How it works

Relaymark connects to Shopify and BigCommerce, builds a profile of every
shopper from orders and browsing events, and runs journeys from a visual
builder. Send-time optimization picks the hour each shopper is most likely
to engage, and a holdout system measures true incremental revenue per flow
rather than last-click attribution.

The shopper profile

Every order, browse, cart event, and message interaction lands on one
profile: what they bought, what they looked at, what they opened, what
they ignored. Segments build from any of it — high-AOV repeat buyers,
browsers who never purchased, subscribers gone quiet for 90 days — and
update in real time as events stream in. The profile is the raw material;
the flows are what it is for.

The flows

Welcome series greet the new subscriber with the story and the first
offer, sequenced over days, exiting early when they buy. Abandoned-cart
recovery picks up the shopper who left at checkout — the highest-intent
moment in e-commerce — with reminders, social proof, and, where you allow
it, an incentive that escalates only if needed. Win-back campaigns target
customers whose purchase cadence has lapsed, and post-purchase flows
handle the unglamorous revenue: order follow-ups, cross-sells timed to
consumption cycles, review requests, replenishment reminders. Each flow
branches on profile data and live behavior in the visual builder — no
code, full logic.

Measurement you can defend

Attribution theater is the industry disease: every tool claims the
revenue the store would have earned anyway. Relaymark's holdout system
keeps a control group per flow and reports true incremental revenue —
what the flow earned beyond what would have happened without it. CFOs
notice the difference. Send-time optimization runs on the same discipline:
the model picks each shopper's likely engagement hour, and its lift is
measured against a control, not asserted.

Templates and testing

Over 900 brands run on Relaymark. Journey templates encode what works —
the site cites a 4-message abandoned-cart series as its most-copied
template — and every flow can A/B test subject lines, timing, and offers.

The template library is organized by vertical and by job: welcome series
for considered purchases, cart recovery for impulse categories,
replenishment flows for consumables. Every template ships with its
branching logic and timing intact; brands customize the voice and keep
the structure. Tests run natively inside flows — subject lines, send
timing, offer depth — with results reported as incremental lift, holdout
included.

SMS, done carefully

SMS is high-attention and high-risk. Relaymark handles consent capture
and record-keeping, quiet hours by recipient timezone, frequency caps
across flows, and automatic opt-out processing. Email and SMS coordinate
inside one journey — the cart reminder texts only if the email went
unopened — so shoppers hear one brand, not two channels competing.

Deliverability

Dedicated sending infrastructure, list hygiene tooling, and automatic
suppression of unengaged addresses keep sender reputation healthy.

Deliverability is treated as product, not support ticket: warm-up
schedules for new sending domains, bounce and complaint monitoring with
automatic throttling, and suppression rules that retire unengaged
addresses before they hurt the sender score. A deliverability specialist
reviews every account's first month of sends.

Integrations

Beyond Shopify and BigCommerce, Relaymark syncs audiences to Meta and
Google ad platforms for suppression and lookalikes, and webhooks stream
every message event to your warehouse. Review-request flows integrate
with Okendo and Judge.me.

The ad-platform sync runs both directions of common sense: suppress
paid spend against shoppers your flows are already converting, and build
lookalikes from your best segments. The webhook stream means your data
team sees every send, open, click, and conversion in the warehouse,
joined to their own models — no export requests, no API archaeology.

Results, measured honestly

Because every flow runs against a holdout, Relaymark publishes what the
category usually hides: aggregate incremental revenue per flow type
across the customer base, updated quarterly. Abandoned-cart recovery
leads, welcome series compound, and win-back is the flow most often
overrated by last-click math — the customers who "came back" mostly
would have anyway, which is exactly what a holdout exists to reveal.
Brands see their own flows benchmarked against vertical medians in the
dashboard, incremental numbers only.

The visual builder, in practice

Journeys are graphs: triggers, waits, branches, and messages. Branch
on anything the profile knows — cart value, category viewed, prior
purchase count, engagement recency — and on live behavior mid-journey:
opened but did not click, clicked but did not buy. Exit rules end the
journey the moment its goal is met, so nobody gets the discount email
after paying full price. Every node shows its live stats in place, and
editing a running flow versions it safely: shoppers mid-journey finish
on the version they started.

Fashion, beauty, food & beverage

The template library leans into the verticals where Relaymark's

Switching from a generic ESP

The generic email platform treats lifecycle as a campaign calendar;
Relaymark treats it as infrastructure. The difference shows up in
three places: flows branch on commerce data the ESP never sees,
measurement runs against holdouts instead of last-click reports, and
deliverability is managed per account rather than left to a shared IP
pool's reputation. The migration team maps your existing automations
during the onboarding call — most translate flow for flow, and the
ones that do not are usually the ones the holdout would have retired
anyway.
