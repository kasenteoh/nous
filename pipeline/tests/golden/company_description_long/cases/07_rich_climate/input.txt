The opportunity

HVAC is typically the largest energy load in an office or retail building,
and most systems run on static schedules programmed years ago. Buildings
heat empty floors, pre-cool on mild days, and fight themselves between
zones.

Walk any office tower at 6 a.m. and the story is the same: the schedule
says occupied at 7, so every floor conditions for a workforce that
arrives — hybrid, now — at 9:30, unevenly, and mostly on floors 4 through
11. The BMS is doing exactly what it was told in 2016. Nobody has time to
retell it every season, and the controls contractor charges by the visit.

How it works

Gridmaple connects to BMS platforms over BACnet — no sensors to install, no
retrofit. Machine-learning models forecast occupancy and weather for each
zone and compute an optimized schedule every 15 minutes. Changes flow back
to the BMS within guardrails the facilities team sets, and every adjustment
is logged and reversible. Utility-rate awareness shifts load away from peak
pricing and demand charges.

Connecting to your BMS

Gridmaple reads and writes over BACnet, the protocol your building
already speaks, and works with the major BMS platforms — Johnson Controls
Metasys, Siemens Desigo, Honeywell, and others. Onboarding is a points
mapping exercise, not a construction project: our team maps zones, air
handlers, and setpoints in the first two weeks, using the as-built
documentation where it exists and discovery where it does not. No sensors
to install, no gateways to rack beyond a single site appliance where the
BMS has no network route.

The optimization loop

For each zone, models trained on your building's own history forecast
occupancy — from badge data, network activity, or learned patterns —
alongside weather and thermal response: how fast this particular floor
warms, how long the chilled water loop takes to answer. Every 15 minutes
Gridmaple computes the schedule and setpoints that meet comfort targets
at minimum cost: later starts on slow days, shallower pre-cooling when
the afternoon will be mild, staggered equipment starts so the building
never spikes demand it will pay for all year.

Guardrails and control

The facilities team defines the envelope: temperature bands per zone,
equipment cycling limits, blackout periods, floors or systems Gridmaple
must not touch. Every adjustment is written through the BMS, logged with
its reason, and reversible — one click returns any zone or the whole
building to its original schedule. Operators see what changed and why in
a daily digest, and comfort complaints can be traced to the exact
adjustment, or ruled out by it.

Utility rates and demand charges

Energy bills are two meters: consumption and peak demand. Gridmaple loads
your tariff — time-of-use windows, demand charge structure, seasonal
rates — and optimizes against dollars, not just kilowatt-hours. Load
shifts away from peak windows, equipment staging avoids coincident
starts, and where utilities run demand-response programs, Gridmaple can
shape participation without comfort excursions.

Results

Customers measure energy savings of 15 to 30 percent on HVAC, verified
against utility meters. Portfolios also use Gridmaple reporting for
emissions disclosures and utility rebate programs.

Measurement and verification

Savings claims in this industry have a credibility problem, so Gridmaple's
are measured the only way that settles arguments: against the utility
meter, weather-normalized, using industry-standard M&V practice. The
baseline is your building's own metered history; the report shows metered
use against the adjusted baseline, month by month. The same data package
supports utility rebate applications — several customers fund part of
their subscription through efficiency programs — and feeds emissions
disclosures with per-building, per-month figures.

Deployment timeline

Weeks one and two: BACnet connection and points mapping. Weeks three and
four: models learn the building in observe mode, recommending but not
writing. Week five onward: closed-loop optimization inside guardrails,
with the facilities team reviewing the daily digest. Full savings ramp
within the first quarter as the models see a season change.

Frequently asked

Does Gridmaple replace my BMS? No. It writes schedules and setpoints to
the BMS you have, within guardrails. Rip-and-replace is exactly what
this product exists to avoid.

What if the internet connection drops? The BMS keeps running the last
written schedule; nothing about your building depends on our uptime.

Can my operators override it? Always — overrides win instantly, are
logged, and inform the next optimization cycle.

Who owns the data? You do. Your points data, your history, exportable
at any time.

Case pattern: the retail portfolio

A portfolio operator with more than a hundred nearly identical sites has
a problem no single-building tool solves: no facilities staff on
premise, and a rollout cost that multiplies by every truck roll. The
Gridmaple pattern for retail is remote-first — BACnet connection through
the existing network, points mapping templated from the prototype store
design, observe mode across the whole estate at once. Optimization
switches on in cohorts, and the M&V report rolls up per region and per
banner. The estate's schedules stop depending on which contractor
commissioned which store in which year.

What Gridmaple needs from you

Three things: BACnet access to the BMS network (read and write, inside
your firewall via the site appliance where needed), twelve months of
utility bills or interval data for the baseline, and your tariff
details — most are in our library already. Occupancy signals improve the
models but are optional: badge feeds and network-activity counts where
you have them, learned patterns where you do not. What Gridmaple does
not need: new sensors, new wiring, or a seat at your capital planning
table.

More from the FAQ

How fast do savings show up? Load shifting starts the day closed-loop
optimization turns on; the full 15 to 30 percent range typically
ramps over the first quarter as models see a season change.

What happens during extreme weather? Guardrails hold — comfort bands
are constraints, not suggestions — and the optimizer works inside
them. Extreme days are where demand-charge avoidance earns the most.

Do tenants notice? The design goal is that they do not: changes stay
inside the comfort band, and complaint rates are tracked per building
as a first-class metric alongside savings.
