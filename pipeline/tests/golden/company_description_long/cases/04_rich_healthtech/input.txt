The problem we solve

Prior authorization is the top administrative burden in specialty care.
Staff spend hours on payer portals and phone calls; patients wait days for
approvals; denials arrive for missing documentation that was sitting in the
chart all along.

The math is grim on both sides. A busy infusion clinic can generate dozens
of authorization requests a day, each with its own payer rules, its own
portal, and its own fax-number fallback. Meanwhile the clinical evidence a
payer wants — the staging, the labs, the prior therapies — already exists
in the chart. The gap between the chart and the portal is where staff
hours, patient days, and revenue disappear.

How it works

Clinicore integrates with Epic, Cerner, and athenahealth. When a clinician
places an order that needs authorization, Clinicore detects it, pulls the
clinical notes and labs that the payer's policy requires, and assembles the
submission. A rules engine encodes payer policies and keeps them current;
our clinical operations team reviews edge cases before anything goes out.
Status updates flow back into the EHR, and staff see one queue instead of
a dozen portals.

Order detection

Clinicore watches the order feed from your EHR and screens each order
against payer and plan: does this CPT code, this payer, this plan require
authorization? Orders that do not require auth are marked cleared —
knowing you do NOT need an authorization is worth as much as filing one.
Orders that do enter the assembly pipeline, with urgency flags carried
through from the order.

Documentation assembly

For each authorization, the rules engine maps the payer's policy to a
checklist of required evidence: diagnosis codes, staging, labs, imaging
reports, prior therapies, clinical notes. Clinicore pulls each item from
the chart and assembles the payer-specific packet. Missing items are
flagged to staff before submission — the denial for "insufficient
documentation" mostly means "the packet went out incomplete," and the
whole point is that it never does.

Submission and tracking

Packets submit electronically wherever the payer supports it, and our
operations team handles portal and fax submission where it does not —
your staff never touches a payer portal either way. Every request is
tracked to a decision: submitted, pended, additional information
requested, approved, denied. Status flows back into the EHR field your
schedulers already check, and the work queue shows exactly which requests
need human attention today.

Denials and appeals

When a denial does happen, Clinicore assembles the appeal from the same
evidence pipeline, attaches the payer's own policy citation, and tracks
the appeal to resolution. Denial analytics show which payers, codes, and
policies generate denials — patterns your revenue cycle team can act on.

The rules engine and the humans

Payer policies change constantly and are published inconsistently. Our
rules engine encodes them as executable checklists, and a dedicated policy
team updates rules as payers publish changes. Anything ambiguous — a new
policy, a conflicting requirement, an unusual clinical situation — routes
to our clinical operations team for human review before submission. The
automation handles the volume; the humans handle the judgment. We publish
our policy coverage by payer and specialty, updated monthly.

Frequently asked

Does Clinicore replace my authorization staff? It replaces their portal
work. Most groups redeploy staff to exception handling, appeals, and
patient communication — the parts that need a human.

Which payers are covered? National payers and the regional payers relevant
to your footprint; coverage by payer and specialty is published and
updated monthly. Where electronic submission is not supported, our
operations team submits on your behalf.

What does my staff actually do day to day? Work one queue: review flagged
items, approve assembled packets where review is configured, and handle
the exceptions Clinicore routes to them.

How is Clinicore priced? Per provider per month, with volume pricing for
groups. No per-transaction fees, no charge for appeals.

About

A day in the authorization queue

8:05 a.m.: the queue shows 34 new orders detected overnight. 26 required
no authorization and are already marked cleared; schedulers see the green
check in the EHR and book them. Six assembled cleanly against payer
policy and await the configured review click. Two are flagged: one
missing a lab the policy requires — the order's clinician gets a task in
the EHR — and one hit a payer policy updated last week, routed to
Clinicore's clinical operations team. By 8:40 the six are submitted with
tracking numbers. By Thursday, four are approved, one is pended with an
information request Clinicore answers from the chart, and the appeal on
last month's denial has a decision date. Nobody logged into a payer
portal.

Urgent authorizations

Urgent-start oncology and cardiac cases follow an expedited path: the
urgency flag carries from the order, packets assemble first in queue,
submission uses the payer's expedited channel where one exists, and our
operations team follows up by phone at the intervals the payer's own
policy promises. Status is visible to the clinic in real time — the
question "where is the auth" has an answer without a phone tree.

Integration notes by EHR

Epic: order detection via standard interfaces, status writeback to the
referral/authorization fields your schedulers already use, no custom
build. Cerner: order and results feeds through standard frameworks, with
document attachment to the encounter. athenahealth: marketplace-listed
integration, live in days. In every case clinicians keep their existing
workflow — the only change they notice is that authorization status
appears without anyone asking.
