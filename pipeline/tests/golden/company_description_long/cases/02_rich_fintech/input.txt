The problem

For companies that sell across several payment providers, reconciliation is
a monthly spreadsheet marathon: exports from each processor, VLOOKUPs,
manual matching of fees, refunds, and chargebacks. Teams routinely spend the
first week of every month closing the previous one.

The worst part is not the volume — it is the exceptions. A payout that nets
out three refunds and a currency conversion. A chargeback that lands two
months after the sale. A processor fee schedule that changed mid-month.
Every one of them is an hour in a spreadsheet, and every close resurfaces
the same ones.

How Ledgerline works

Connect your payment processors and bank accounts through read-only
integrations. Ledgerline's matching engine pairs each payout with its
underlying transactions, nets out processor fees, currency conversion, and
refunds, and posts journal entries to NetSuite, QuickBooks, or Xero. Rules
you accept once are applied automatically the next month. Anything the
engine cannot match with confidence lands in a review queue with the
evidence attached.

The matching engine

Matching starts at the payout: for every deposit that hits your bank,
Ledgerline reconstructs the set of charges, refunds, chargebacks, and fees
inside it, using processor reports and your invoice data. Multi-currency
payouts are decomposed with the processor's own conversion rate, so FX
differences post to the right account instead of vanishing into a rounding
line. Partial payments, over-payments, and invoices settled across two
payouts are first-class cases, not edge cases.

Every match carries a confidence score. High-confidence matches post
automatically under rules your team has approved; everything else queues
for review. Nothing is ever posted silently — the audit trail records
which rule matched, who approved it, and when.

The review queue

The review queue is where your team's judgment gets captured. Each
exception shows the payout line, the candidate transactions, and the
mismatch — amount, date, currency, or missing counterpart — with a
suggested resolution. Accept the suggestion, and Ledgerline offers to turn
it into a rule; next month the same pattern resolves itself. Customers
typically see their exception queue shrink by more than half within three
closes as rules accumulate.

Journal entries and the close

Ledgerline posts summarized or transaction-level journal entries to
NetSuite, QuickBooks, or Xero — your choice per account. Fee expense,
FX gain/loss, refunds, and chargeback reserves each map to the GL accounts
you configure once. A close dashboard tracks each processor's status:
transactions ingested, matched percentage, exceptions open, entries
posted. When every lane is green, the reconciliation is done — and your
auditor gets an export with every match's evidence attached.

Product tour: from payout to journal entry

Day one, Ledgerline ingests your processor reports and bank feed. Each
payout appears as a card: gross charges, fees, refunds, chargebacks, FX,
net deposit. Green cards matched automatically under an approved rule;
amber cards need review. Open an amber card and the evidence is laid out —
the invoice, the processor line, the delta — with the suggested resolution
at the top. One click accepts it; a second click makes it a rule. At the
end of the lane, the journal entry preview shows exactly what will post to
your ledger, line by line, before you approve it. Month two, the amber
cards are fewer. Month three, your close checklist starts at "review
exceptions" instead of "download exports."

Guide: FX rounding policy for multi-currency payouts

When a euro charge settles into a dollar payout, three numbers disagree:
the rate the processor applied, the rate your ledger expected, and the
rate the bank statement implies. The guide covers choosing a single source
of truth per processor, posting the residual to a dedicated FX gain/loss
account, and setting a materiality threshold so the queue is not cluttered
with one-cent deltas. Ledgerline applies the policy automatically once
configured — the point is that the policy is a decision, not a hope.
